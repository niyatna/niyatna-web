Niyatna formation starts with company intent, not tooling. The goal is to understand how work should move through people, agents, memory, permissions, and proof before automation is introduced.

---

## Phase 1: map company intent

Identify the work that should become askable, assignable, remembered, automated, and proven.

* **Intent mapping:** Clarify the company outcomes that matter.
* **Drag audit:** Find recurring work, repeated questions, handoff delays, and review bottlenecks.
* **Boundary design:** Decide which actions can move automatically, which require review, and which should stay blocked.

## Phase 2: define the operating map

Niyatna needs a company map before agents can act safely.

Define:

* roles and departments;
* authorized requesters;
* review and approval owners;
* external partners or clients with scoped access;
* SOPs, standards, and recurring workflows;
* evidence required before work is considered complete.

## Phase 3: create the first OpenIntent flows

Start with a narrow set of repeatable requests.

Each flow should include:

* the request shape;
* context and source materials;
* permission boundaries;
* assigned human or agent path;
* acceptance criteria;
* proof and report-back format.

## Phase 4: connect memory, route, and proof

Once the first flows are clear, Niyatna connects the system surfaces:

* **Memory** provides relevant company context.
* **Route** chooses the right human, agent, model, or tool path.
* **HQ** tracks owned work and review gates.
* **Proof of Intent** records evidence and final status.

## Phase 5: expand deliberately

After the first workflows prove useful, expand by department or operating area. Niyatna should grow as a governed company system, not as a pile of disconnected automations.
